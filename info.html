What to know:

Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS) is a common hormonal condition that affects many women during their reproductive years. It happens when a woman's hormones become unbalanced. Because of this, the ovaries may develop small fluid-filled sacs called cysts.

How it can affect?

These cysts contain immature eggs that do not develop or release properly. This can affect a woman's menstrual cycle and fertility. PCOS can also run in families. Studies show that about 10–13% of women around the world have PCOS, but many cases are not diagnosed.

PCOS can affect both the body and emotions of a woman. Women with PCOS may have a higher risk of some health problems such as insulin resistance, type 2 diabetes, and obesity.

  • Irregular or missed menstrual periods
  • Difficulty getting pregnant
  • Excess facial or body hair
  • Acne or oily skin

Hormonal changes may also affect a woman’s mood. Some women with PCOS may feel anxious, stressed, or depressed. These changes can affect their daily life and relationships.

Health Risks of PCOS

If PCOS is not managed properly, it may lead to other health problems:

• OBSTETRIC COMPLICATIONS

A hormonal imbalance (high androgens, low progesterone), insulin resistance, and chronic inflammation, leading to a higher risk of miscarriage, gestational diabetes, hypertension/preeclampsia, and preterm birth.

• DEPRESSION

A risk via hormonal, metabolic, and inflammatory pathways. Major depression, recurrent depression, and suicide attempts.

• INFERTILITY

Most women with PCOS have elevated levels of luteinizing hormone and reduced levels of follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH), coupled with elevated levels of androgens and insulin—manifest as oligomenorrhea or amenorrhea (infrequent or lack of menstruation). Underproduction of estrogen and overproduction of androgens (testosterone, dehydroepiandrosterone, and androstenedione) by the ovaries can result in a number of additional clinical features, including tiny cysts on the surface of the ovaries (polycysts) and hair and skin symptoms.

• ENDOMETRIAL CANCER

There are two causes:

Too Many Androgens: Having too many androgens in your body.

Too Much Insulin: High insulin levels are connected to endometrial cancer in people living with PCOS, causing cells to grow too fast.

• METABOLIC DYSFUNCTION-ASSOCIATED STEATOTIC LIVER DISEASE

Formerly known as nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD); MASLD has been associated with androgen excess and insulin resistance. This leads to increased lipolysis and a subsequent rise in FFA flow from adipose tissue to the liver, predisposing women with PCOS to liver disease.

ADVICE

Taking care of your health is very important. Even though some illnesses like PCOS can be hereditary, it does not mean that nothing can be done about it. With proper care and healthy habits, the symptoms can still be managed.

One important thing is maintaining a healthy lifestyle. Eating balanced meals, drinking enough water, and getting regular exercise can help improve overall health and may help control some symptoms of PCOS. Simple activities like walking, stretching, or light workouts can already make a difference if done regularly.

Keep track of your menstrual cycle, paying attention to changes in your body, such as irregular periods, sudden acne, or unusual hair growth can help you notice possible symptoms early. If these signs continue, it is better to consult a doctor or healthcare professional for proper advice.

Aside from physical health, emotional health also matters. Hormonal changes may sometimes affect a person's mood. Having support from family and friends can help, and talking to someone you trust will make it easier to deal with stress or worries.

In the end, being aware of your body and taking care of your health can make a big difference. PCOS may be a long-term condition, but with the right care, medical guidance, and healthy daily habits, many women are still able to live healthy and active lives.
